This state-of-the-art report surveys modern approaches to non-rigid 3D shape correspondence, spanning spectral, combinatorial, and deformation-based methods. It is intended as a structured entry point for researchers, practitioners, and students interested in the foundations, recent advances, and open challenges of the field.

Abstract

Estimating correspondences between deformed shape instances is a long-standing problem in computer graphics; numerous applications, from texture transfer to statistical modelling, rely on recovering an accurate correspondence map. Many methods have thus been proposed to tackle this challenging problem from varying perspectives, depending on the downstream application. This state-of-the-art report is geared towards researchers, practitioners, and students seeking to understand recent trends and advances in the field. We categorise developments into three paradigms: spectral methods based on functional maps, combinatorial formulations that impose discrete constraints, and deformation-based methods that directly recover a global alignment. Each school of thought offers different advantages and disadvantages, which we discuss throughout the report. Meanwhile, we highlight the latest developments in each area and suggest new potential research directions. Finally, we provide an overview of emerging challenges and opportunities in this growing field, including the recent use of vision foundation models for zero-shot correspondence and the particularly challenging task of matching partial shapes.

Part 1: Shape correspondence problem

(a) Per-vertex feature functions are computed on two non-rigidly deformed shapes visualised by colour. When these features are sufficiently descriptive, a correspondence mapping can be derived.
(b) Dense correspondences computed between two non-rigidly deformed shapes, demonstrating geometric and semantic alignment.

In 3D geometry processing, a shape is typically defined as a two-dimensional surface embedded in three-dimensional space, often discretised as a triangular mesh with vertices \( X_\mathcal{M} \), edges \( E_\mathcal{M} \), and faces \( T_\mathcal{M} \). Alternatively, shapes may be represented as unstructured point clouds. Given two shapes \( \mathcal{M} \) and \( \mathcal{N} \), the goal of shape correspondence is to determine which points on \( \mathcal{M} \) match semantically or geometrically to points on \( \mathcal{N} \). This relationship is expressed as a point-wise map \( \Pi_{\mathcal{M}\mathcal{N}} : \mathcal{M} \rightarrow \mathcal{N} \), assigning each point on the source shape to a corresponding point on the target shape. We use the terms correspondence and matching interchangeably. We focus on dense correspondences, where every point on the source shape is matched. Formally, for \( n_{\mathcal{M}} \leqslant n_{\mathcal{N}} \), the map \( \Pi_{\mathcal{M}\mathcal{N}} \) is injective: \( \Pi_{\mathcal{M}\mathcal{N}} \mathbf{1} = \mathbf{1}, \; \Pi_{\mathcal{M}\mathcal{N}}^\top \mathbf{1} \leqslant \mathbf{1} \), where \( \mathbf{1} \) denotes the all-ones vector.

A central goal in correspondence estimation is to quantify similarity between shapes in a tractable form. Given shapes \( \mathcal{M} \) and \( \mathcal{N} \), per-vertex descriptors \( f_\mathcal{M} \in \mathbb{R}^{n_\mathcal{M} \times d} \) and \( f_\mathcal{N} \in \mathbb{R}^{n_\mathcal{N} \times d} \) encode geometric or semantic properties, either via hand-crafted heuristics or learned models. The descriptor preservation principle assumes that corresponding points share similar features: \( f_\mathcal{M} \approx \Pi_{\mathcal{M}\mathcal{N}} f_\mathcal{N} \). Classical descriptors include extrinsic signals (e.g. coordinates, normals) and intrinsic ones (e.g. curvature, HKS, WKS), each with inherent limitations such as lack of isometric invariance, sensitivity to noise, or ambiguity under symmetries. Recent approaches instead learn descriptors from data, producing more expressive and task-specific features, though challenges remain due to ambiguities, partiality, and discretisation artefacts. A naive strategy is to recover correspondences via nearest-neighbour matching: \( \underset{\Pi_{\mathcal{M}\mathcal{N}} \in \{0,1\}^{n_\mathcal{M} \times n_\mathcal{N}}}{\arg\min} \| f_\mathcal{M} - \Pi_{\mathcal{M}\mathcal{N}} f_\mathcal{N} \|_F^2 \), but without additional constraints this often leads to geometrically inconsistent mappings. Consequently, modern methods incorporate structural priors and geometric constraints to obtain accurate correspondences.

Correspondence evaluation

(a) Color transfer provides an overview of the global correspondence structure.
(b) High-frequency texture transfer can reveal small local misalignments.
(c) Deformation transfer can similarly visualise small topological inconsistencies caused by inaccurate correspondences.

Once a correspondence map \( \Pi_{\mathcal{M}\mathcal{N}} \) is recovered, it can be evaluated using several complementary criteria. Accuracy is typically measured against ground-truth correspondences using normalised geodesic error, from which standard metrics such as mean geodesic error (MGE), percentage of correct keypoints (PCK), and area under the PCK curve (AUC) are derived.

Beyond accuracy, smoothness evaluates geometric consistency, for example via geodesic distortion, Dirichlet energy, or conformal distortion, which penalise irregular or non-local mappings. Coverage measures how completely the target shape is utilised, often through the un-coverage rate, while bijectivity assesses one-to-one behaviour using inverse consistency and orientation preservation criteria.

In practice, visualisation (e.g. colour or texture transfer, deformation transfer) provides qualitative insight into correspondence quality. Importantly, downstream performance often imposes additional requirements beyond pure accuracy: applications such as deformation transfer, statistical shape modelling, or medical analysis demand correspondences that are not only accurate but also smooth, consistent, and semantically meaningful.

Part 2: Spectral methods

Visualisation of the functional map framework as an analogy to Fourier analysis. Functions \( f_{\mathcal{M}} \), \( f_{\mathcal{N}} \) on shapes \( \mathcal{M} \), \( \mathcal{N} \) are represented in the Laplace–Beltrami basis \( \phi \), and the functional map \( C_{\mathcal{MN}} \) transfers their coefficients \( a \rightarrow b \), encoding point-wise correspondences \( \Pi_{\mathcal{NM}} \) in a compact \( k \times k \) matrix.

Spectral methods provide a powerful framework for correspondence by leveraging intrinsic properties of shapes that are invariant to isometric deformations. Central to this approach is the Laplace–Beltrami operator (LBO), a generalisation of the Laplace operator to surfaces, defined as \( \Delta f = \operatorname{div}(\nabla f) \). In the discrete setting, it is commonly represented using the cotangent Laplacian \( L_{\mathcal{M}} = A_{\mathcal{M}}^{-1} W_{\mathcal{M}} \), where \( W_{\mathcal{M}} \) encodes cotangent weights and \( A_{\mathcal{M}} \) contains local area elements. The eigendecomposition of the LBO, \( \Delta_{\mathcal{M}} \phi_i = \lambda_i \phi_i \), yields eigenfunctions that form an intrinsic basis ordered by frequency, providing a natural representation for functions on the surface.

Building on this spectral representation, the functional map framework expresses correspondences as linear operators between function spaces rather than point-wise mappings. Given descriptor functions \( f_{\mathcal{M}} \), \( f_{\mathcal{N}} \) and spectral bases \( \phi_{\mathcal{M}} \), \( \phi_{\mathcal{N}} \), the functional map \( C_{\mathcal{MN}} \in \mathbb{R}^{k \times k} \) is estimated by solving \( C_{\mathcal{MN}} = \arg\min_{C} \| C \phi_{\mathcal{M}}^{\dagger} f_{\mathcal{M}} - \phi_{\mathcal{N}}^{\dagger} f_{\mathcal{N}} \|_F^2 \), effectively reducing the high-dimensional matching problem to a compact coefficient matrix. The corresponding point-wise map can then be recovered via \( \Pi_{\mathcal{NM}} \phi_{\mathcal{N}} \approx \phi_{\mathcal{M}} C_{\mathcal{NM}} \), linking spatial and spectral representations. This formulation enables efficient and elegant correspondence estimation and serves as the foundation for both classical refinement methods and modern learning-based approaches.

Axiomatic methods start from an initial, often noisy correspondence and refine it using geometric constraints. They leverage the relationship between functional maps and point-wise maps, enabling iterative optimisation that enforces smoothness, bijectivity, and geometric consistency. While these approaches provide strong theoretical foundations, they often rely on assumptions such as near-isometric deformations, limiting their robustness in real-world settings. Consequently, recent work integrates these principles into learning-based frameworks for improved performance.
